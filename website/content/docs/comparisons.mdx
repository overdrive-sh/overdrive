Overdrive folds into one Rust binary what other platforms assemble from
separate parts: the control plane, the load balancer, the service mesh, the
certificate authority, secrets, and observability. Kubernetes reaches the same
capability set by composing etcd, a CNI plugin, a mesh, cert-manager, and a
metrics stack. Nomad ships a single scheduler binary but leans on Consul and
Vault for mesh and secrets. Fly.io runs a comparable architecture internally —
microVMs, a Rust edge proxy, a gossip-replicated catalog — but as a managed
product you deploy onto, not one you operate.

This page compares the four on architecture and design philosophy. It is not a
maturity claim.

<Callout title="Read this first: Overdrive is early">
	Overdrive is at Phase 1 — single-node and pre-production. Kubernetes, Nomad,
	and Fly.io are production systems running real workloads at scale today.
	Where this page compares Overdrive's design against a competitor's shipped
	behaviour, it is comparing an intended architecture to a proven one. Where a
	competitor is more mature, more proven, or simply the right choice, the page
	says so plainly.
</Callout>

## Why the design looks different

Overdrive starts from a different question than the others. Kubernetes was
designed in 2014, on the primitives that existed then: container runtimes,
iptables, userspace proxies, sidecars. Its architecture is sound for that era.
Then the primitives changed.

Stable eBPF and XDP put a programmable load balancer in the kernel. kTLS moves
encryption off the userspace proxy. SPIFFE gives every workload a cryptographic
identity. WASM with the Component Model makes a sandboxed, language-agnostic
extension model practical. openraft and Corrosion make the consistency split
tractable in a single process. None of these were production-ready when
Kubernetes set its shape.

Build on the 2026 primitives instead of retrofitting them, and the parts that
Kubernetes keeps separate collapse into one binary. That is the bet. It is a
design bet, and it is unproven at scale — but it is the reason the comparison
rows below come out the way they do.

## Versus Kubernetes

**What Kubernetes is, and where it shines.** Kubernetes is the declarative
container orchestrator the industry standardized on. Its control plane —
kube-apiserver, etcd, kube-scheduler, and the controller-manager — drives a
reconciliation model: controllers watch desired state and act to make actual
state match. Its strengths are real and hard to beat. Every major cloud offers
a managed Kubernetes. The operator ecosystem numbers in the thousands. Helm,
GitOps, and the CNCF tooling landscape are mature. The hiring pool is the
deepest in the industry. If the requirement is "boring, supported everywhere,
and easy to staff," Kubernetes is the answer.

**How Overdrive differs.** Overdrive keeps the reconciliation model Kubernetes
proved and changes three things underneath it.

- **The reconciler is a pure function.** A Kubernetes operator is a long-running
  Go program with broad cluster privileges. An Overdrive reconciler is a single
  synchronous, pure function: it takes the desired and observed state plus its own
  prior view, and returns the actions to take and the next view to persist. No I/O,
  no clock, no database handle. That purity is what lets the control plane be replayed deterministically
  under simulation, and it is why the reconciler's correctness can be expressed
  as invariants the test harness checks mechanically.
- **One binary, no etcd, no CNI, no mesh, no cert-manager.** Kubernetes assembles
  networking from a CNI plugin, mTLS from a mesh add-on or SPIRE, and certificates
  from cert-manager. Overdrive builds the load balancer, the mesh, and the
  certificate authority into the same binary that runs the control plane.
- **All workload types are first-class.** Kubernetes runs containers; VMs need
  KubeVirt and stronger isolation needs Kata Containers — both add-ons. Overdrive
  runs exec processes, microVMs, full VMs, unikernels, and WASM modules through
  one driver interface, one identity model, and one dataplane.

**The honest counterweight.** Sidecarless, eBPF-based service mesh is no longer
a Kubernetes weakness — Cilium and Istio's ambient mode (GA in November 2024,
with a per-node Rust proxy for L4 mTLS) both ship it today, in production. The
difference Overdrive can claim is integration, not invention: the mesh is in the
binary and identity-bearing kernel mTLS is the default, with no add-on to install.
On everything maturity confers — ecosystem, scale, managed availability —
Kubernetes wins, and will for a long time.

<Callout type="info" title="When to choose which">
	Choose **Kubernetes** when you need a proven platform, a managed offering from
	your cloud, a large hiring pool, or one of the thousands of existing operators.
	Choose **Overdrive** when operational simplicity matters more than ecosystem
	maturity — one binary instead of etcd, a CNI plugin, a mesh, and cert-manager —
	and you want built-in mTLS and mixed workload types (containers *and* microVMs
	*and* WASM under one model). The cost is pre-production software.
</Callout>

## Versus Nomad

**What Nomad is, and where it shines.** Nomad is the closest competitor in
spirit. It is a single binary that runs as either a server or a client, needs no
external coordination service, and schedules workloads by bin-packing them onto
clients subject to job constraints. Its task-driver model runs Docker, raw exec,
QEMU, Java, and community drivers including Firecracker. Multi-region federation
is built in. Nomad is the proven benchmark for self-hosted orchestration that is
simpler than Kubernetes — and it ships today.

**How Overdrive differs.** Both platforms value operational simplicity; they
draw the binary's boundary in different places.

- **Mesh and secrets are inside, not alongside.** Nomad's service mesh comes from
  Consul Connect and its secrets from Vault — both separate HashiCorp products you
  deploy and operate next to Nomad. Overdrive's mesh, certificate authority, and
  identity live in the same binary as the scheduler. The "single binary" claim
  holds for the full stack, not just the scheduler.
- **Identity is structural.** On Nomad, workload mTLS is something you add by
  wiring in Consul Connect. On Overdrive, every workload gets a short-lived SPIFFE
  certificate and east-west traffic is encrypted at the socket layer by default —
  it cannot be turned off.
- **Correctness is tested by simulation.** Overdrive's nondeterminism — clock,
  network, randomness, storage — sits behind injectable traits, so partition,
  timing, and crash-ordering bugs are reproducible from a seed. This is a property
  shared with serious database projects, not with general-purpose orchestrators.

**The honest counterweight.** Nomad's simplicity is shipped and battle-tested;
Overdrive's is designed. Nomad's driver ecosystem is broad and proven in
production now. If the requirement is "simpler than Kubernetes, self-hosted, and
running this quarter," Nomad is the safer choice today, and adding Consul and
Vault is a well-trodden path.

<Callout type="info" title="When to choose which">
	Choose **Nomad** when you want self-hosted orchestration that is simpler than
	Kubernetes and proven in production, and you are comfortable running Consul and
	Vault for mesh and secrets. Choose **Overdrive** when you want that simplicity
	*without* the extra products — mesh, identity, and secrets folded into one
	binary — and can accept that it is not yet production-ready.
</Callout>

## Versus Fly.io

**What Fly.io is, and where it shines.** Fly.io is a managed platform for running
applications close to users. Application code runs in Firecracker microVMs; a
per-server Rust edge proxy (`fly-proxy`) terminates TLS and routes requests by
BGP Anycast to the nearest instance; a global WireGuard mesh carries private
traffic between regions. Its service catalog runs on **Corrosion**, a Rust program
that propagates a SQLite database by gossip using CRDT semantics. Fly's strengths
are a proven global edge, a polished deploy experience (`fly deploy`), and the
fact that you operate none of it.

**How Overdrive differs.** This is the most architecturally similar of the three,
which makes the differences precise. Overdrive uses the *same* Corrosion component
for its observation store, and it arrives at the same topology Fly runs in
production — per-region strong consistency for intent, global gossip for observed
state. It diverges on two axes.

- **The dataplane is in the kernel, not userspace.** Fly routes east-west traffic
  through `fly-proxy` and the WireGuard mesh. Overdrive uses XDP for load balancing
  and sockops with kTLS for encryption — the work happens in the kernel, and
  same-host delivery skips the network stack entirely via a cgroup socket hook.
- **Identity is per-workload, not per-machine.** Fly's documented trust model is
  machine-level WireGuard keys plus TLS termination at the edge proxy. Overdrive
  binds a SPIFFE identity to each workload and carries it on every east-west packet.
  (Fly does not document a built-in per-workload mTLS identity; this is the absence
  of a documented feature, not a claim that Fly cannot do it.)
- **You run it.** This is the largest difference, and it cuts both ways. Fly is a
  product; Overdrive is software you deploy and operate.

**The honest counterweight.** Fly's edge is real, global, and proven; Overdrive's
multi-region design is exactly that — a design. Fly's deploy experience is mature
and Overdrive's is not. If the goal is "put an application near users without
running infrastructure," Fly is the product built for that, and Overdrive is not
a managed offering.

<Callout type="info" title="When to choose which">
	Choose **Fly.io** when you want a managed platform with a proven global edge and
	a fast path from code to a deployed app, and you do not want to run
	infrastructure. Choose **Overdrive** when you want to run the platform yourself,
	want the dataplane and identity in the kernel rather than a userspace proxy, and
	are building rather than buying.
</Callout>

## Where each competitor wins

It is worth stating the trade-off without softening it. Overdrive's
differentiators are structural — integration, built-in identity, a unified
workload model, simulation-tested correctness. They are not maturity. On
maturity, the competitors win:

- **Kubernetes** wins on ecosystem, scale, managed availability, and hiring.
- **Fly.io** wins on a proven global edge and deploy experience, fully managed.
- **Nomad** wins on shipped, proven simplicity for self-hosted orchestration.

Overdrive's bet is that the 2026 primitives make a structurally simpler, more
secure, more verifiable platform possible — one binary instead of a stack, mTLS
by default instead of by add-on, every workload type under one model, and
correctness checked by simulation from day one. That bet is not yet proven in
production. If maturity is the deciding factor, choose one of the platforms
above. If the architecture is what you are evaluating, that is what this page is
for.
