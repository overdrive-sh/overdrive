Running workloads in production usually means assembling three or four
independent systems — a scheduler, a service mesh, a policy engine, a secrets
broker — and then spending the next year teaching them to agree with each other.
Each speaks its own dialect of "desired state," each has its own failure modes,
and the seams between them are where the outages live.

Overdrive starts from a different premise: the orchestrator, the dataplane, and
the policy layer are not separate products that need integrating — they are
three views of one piece of state. We split that state cleanly into **intent**
(what the cluster should look like) and **observation** (what it actually looks
like right now), enforce the split structurally rather than by convention, and
let a single control plane reconcile the two.

## One boundary, enforced by types

The intent/observation split is the load-bearing decision. Intent is
strongly-consistent, authored through typed actions, and the single source of
truth. Observation is eventually-consistent, gossiped between nodes, and never
written back into intent. Because the two live behind different store traits,
crossing the boundary is a compile error, not a code-review note.

That discipline is what lets the rest of the platform stay simple. The scheduler
reads intent and writes intent. The reconcilers read both and converge actual
toward desired. The dataplane is just another observed surface that policy
verdicts flow into. There is no integration layer because there is nothing to
integrate.

## Correctness you can reproduce

A platform that places workloads, rotates certificates, and enforces network
policy cannot be tested by hoping the happy path holds. Every source of
nondeterminism in Overdrive's core — the clock, the network, entropy, the
dataplane — sits behind a port trait, so the same logic runs against a
deterministic simulation harness that can replay any failing schedule
bit-for-bit from a seed.

The result is a platform where "it works on my cluster" is replaced by "here is
the seed that reproduces the bug." That is the property we wanted, and it is the
reason the whole stack is built the way it is.
